Tool eccentricity compensation in milling

86%↓
Peak cutting-force variation cut from 101 N to 14 N on a 3-axis CNC, converging in 2-3 spindle iterations from force feedback alone, with no dedicated hardware.
Hardware & instrumentation

Overview

Tool eccentricity (radial runout) offsets the cutter's geometric center from the spindle axis, so each tooth takes an uneven chip load. That force imbalance hurts surface finish, accelerates tool wear, and is especially costly in finishing. Manual tramming reduces runout but is slow and cannot adapt in-process.

Approach

The eccentricity shows up as a periodic motion of the tool center at the spindle frequency. The method commands a small spindle-synchronized circular micro-trajectory through the machine's existing X-Y feed drives to cancel it.

Open-loop calibration is unreliable because feed-drive dynamics distort the injected motion, so the compensation amplitude and phase are tuned on-machine from force or vibration feedback, minimizing a spectral cost on the spindle-synchronous harmonics.

The tuning evolved from extremum-seeking (fully model-free) to a convex-optimization formulation that exploits a minimal eccentricity insight for reliable, fast convergence.

What is novel

  • Uses the machine's own feed drives instead of dedicated hardware or offline tramming.
  • Infers eccentricity from force/vibration, with no direct runout measurement.
  • Convex formulation gives fast, repeatable convergence and is robust to setup/phase changes.

Results

Using force feedback, the spindle-synchronized compensation converges in as few as 2-3 iterations (10-15 spindle revolutions). Peak-to-peak normal cutting force dropped from 101 N to 14 N, about an 86% reduction (roughly 7.2x lower), validated cutting a steel alloy with a 4-tooth, 30-degree helix tool at 1200 rpm.
