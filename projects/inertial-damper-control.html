Overview

Fast point-to-point motion excites the structural modes of machine tools and industrial robots, producing overshoot and residual vibration that force a choice between speed and precision: either move slowly, or wait out the settling. Active inertial dampers (a reaction mass on a voice-coil or linear-motor actuator) help, but a feedback-only damper reacts after vibration appears — it can never remove the initial overshoot, and pushing its gains runs the compact actuator into stroke and force limits.

Approach

The damper applies a task-specific, pre-scheduled feedforward force profile — learned from measured vibration over repeated executions of the motion — on top of its feedback controller. Since production moves repeat, the learned profile can act at the exact instant the excitation occurs, eliminating vibration the feedback loop would only react to.

The feedforward is parameterized with filtered B-spline basis functions: a handful of control points give smooth actuator signals and a small, fast optimization, and actuator stroke and force constraints are enforced directly inside the learning problem — so the solution is always realizable by compact damper hardware. The scheme needs no tight integration with the host NC, which keeps deployment practical.

What is novel

  • Pre-scheduled, task-specific feedforward removes the initial overshoot that feedback-only damping fundamentally cannot.
  • Low-dimensional B-spline parameterization makes learning fast, smooth, and repeatable.
  • Constraint-aware optimization keeps commands within stroke/force limits — enabling smaller, cheaper damper hardware for the same performance.

Results

On a feed-drive testbed, the learned feedforward converged in about 10 iterations and reduced peak positioning error by 87% (12 µm to 1.6 µm), reaching the ±2 µm tolerance band essentially immediately instead of waiting out a settling transient — while a conventional velocity-feedback damper repeatedly saturated its stroke and force limits on the same move.
