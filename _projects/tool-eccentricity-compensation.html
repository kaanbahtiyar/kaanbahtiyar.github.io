Active runout control

Overview

Tool eccentricity and cutter runout offset the tool's geometric center from its axis of rotation, so each cutting edge removes a slightly different chip. That once-per-revolution force imbalance roughens the surface finish, concentrates wear on the most-loaded tooth, and leads to premature tool failure — a costly problem in finishing, and in low-speed cutting of hard alloys such as titanium and Inconel. The standard countermeasure, manual dial-gauge tramming during tool setup, is slow, depends on operator skill, and cannot react to thermal drift or wear once the cut starts.

Approach

Kinematically, eccentricity makes the tool center trace a small circle at the spindle rotation frequency. The method cancels it by commanding an equal-and-opposite micro-circular trajectory through the machine's existing X–Y feed drives, injected into the servo position loop at 5 kHz on top of the nominal toolpath — no added actuators, no dedicated hardware.

The catch is that feed-drive dynamics distort any injected motion, so an open-loop, pre-calibrated command is unreliable. Instead, the compensation amplitude and phase are tuned on-machine from force or accelerometer feedback, by minimizing the spindle-synchronous spectral content that eccentricity produces. The tuning evolved from a fully model-free extremum-seeking scheme to a convex optimization with Newton-type updates: the problem's Hessian is constant, so the optimum is unique and convergence is fast and repeatable. A single probing move identifies the drive response, after which the algorithm inherently compensates for feed-drive dynamics — the practical limit becomes servo torque, not tracking bandwidth, which makes the method portable across machine tools.

What is novel

  • Uses the machine's own feed drives — no dedicated compensation hardware and no offline tramming.
  • Senses eccentricity indirectly from force or vibration spectra; no direct runout measurement is required, and with force feedback the spindle-frequency harmonic alone is sufficient.
  • Convex formulation with a constant Hessian guarantees a unique optimum and converges in 2–3 updates, robust to setup and phase changes.
  • Designed to live inside the NC: updates are triggered once per revolution, which a spindle encoder already provides. Patent pending with an industrial partner.

Results

In steel-alloy milling on a 3-axis CNC, force-based tuning converged in as few as 2–3 iterations (10–15 spindle revolutions) and cut the peak cutting-force variation from 101 N to 14 N — about 86%. The behavior was validated with 2-, 3-, and 4-tooth cutters and across spindle speeds; an accelerometer-only variant (no dynamometer on the machine) also converges by including higher harmonics in the cost, in roughly 10 iterations.

Because the correction balances the chip load rather than just smoothing a signal, the payoff compounds over a production run: even flank wear across all teeth, fewer premature tool changes, and a finish that no longer carries the once-per-revolution eccentricity pattern.
