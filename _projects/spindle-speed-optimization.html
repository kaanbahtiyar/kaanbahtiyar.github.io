Spindle speed control for chatter mitigation

Overview

Milling vibration comes in two forms: self-excited chatter (an instability that grows until it scraps the part) and periodic forced vibration (stable, but it still degrades surface finish and part tolerance). Both shorten tool life and can damage the spindle. The standard fixes have real costs on the shop floor: model-based planning with stability lobe diagrams requires modal testing and re-identification for every tool–holder–machine combination, while the common chatter-frequency speed-regulation rule stabilizes the cut but parks the spindle right next to a structural resonance — amplifying forced vibration and producing large surface location errors (in our tests, a small speed error near resonance swung the surface location error from a 25 µm overcut to a 50 µm undercut).

Approach

Spindle-speed selection is posed as an online optimization running while the machine cuts: a vibration cost is computed from accelerometer data once per spindle revolution, and the speed is updated in the direction that reduces it. An extremum-seeking controller estimates the cost gradient directly from measured data — no process model, no stability lobes, no modal testing. A dither-guided variant probes with a small speed perturbation; a dither-free variant fits the gradient by least squares from a buffer of recent speed–cost pairs.

Deployment is retrofit-free. The controller runs on an external real-time board and talks to a production CNC entirely through interfaces every machine already has: acceleration is sampled at 10 kHz, speed commands are written to the machine's spindle-override input via PLC functions at 2 kHz, and a once-per-revolution optical trigger keeps the algorithm synchronized to the spindle. Nothing inside the CNC is modified.

What is novel

  • Optimizes total vibration — forced plus chatter — instead of only chasing the chatter frequency, so it avoids the classic trap of stabilizing the cut at a resonance-adjacent speed.
  • Fully model-free and on-machine: no stability-lobe identification, no modal testing, transfers across tools and machines.
  • Converges to low surface-location-error operating points, where the conventional strategy tends to land in the most SLE-sensitive speed regions.
  • Runs through the standard spindle-override interface, which makes it practical to deploy on existing machines.
Real-time spindle-speed adaptation during a milling test: the controller activates after chatter develops and drives the vibration down.

Results

In milling tests on a 3-axis CNC (steel alloy, multiple cutters and start conditions), the controller eliminated chatter and cut RMS vibration by 75–85% — for example from 131 to 20 m/s² on a rigid workpiece and from 129 to 22 m/s² on a flexible fixture. Benchmarked head-to-head against the conventional chatter-frequency strategy at its own recommended speeds, the converged operating points showed 57–66% lower vibration (21 vs. 49 m/s² and 25 vs. 73 m/s²).

The benefit is not limited to chatter: starting from an already-stable cut, the optimizer still halved RMS vibration (8 to 4 m/s²) and reduced the mean normal cutting force by 13% — a direct tool-life benefit — simply by finding a better speed.
